Editor's Desk
As the dust settles on the tumultuous multitude of opinions about the recently concluded Assembly elections in the five states in India, the time is now ripe to take stock of the motifs and messages that were communicated by major political parties that either won or were vanquished. Retention of the BJP Government in all the four states where they were already in power, and the unexpected overwhelming majority of Aam Admi Party in the Punjab Legislative Assembly will lead to potential adjustments in the Policy landscape. These will unfold in the coming months.

This month, out of 16 states (included in our State Championship program), 11 state governments have announced their budgets. We have included key highlights and announcements made by them in their State Budgets for 2022-23.

Major developments that took place in the automobile sector included the Draft Electric Vehicle Ecosystem Policy by the West Bengal Government (Our suggestions were well received by the WB Government), draft notification for registration and function of Vehicle scrap facility, draft notification to formalize the movement of international non-transport vehicle among others. The amendment in motor vehicles rules, 2021 to bring digitization and transparency in a vehicle scrap facility is an important step towards driving making the scrappage facility.

NITI Aayog’s comprehensive analysis of export preparedness of different state as well as the status of the country as a whole across various parameters have been the highlight of the month. NITI Aayog has come up with the Export Preparedness Index, ranking the States and identifying different pillars which provide an impetus to exports from a particular region. This index is going to help the States in devising and refining policies to boost exports.

On March 24, 2022, the UN Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD) released its "Tapering in a Time of Conflict" report to say that the global output growth is likely to decline from the earlier estimate of 3.6% to 2.6% in 2022 (January-December) – a 1% fall. To a great extent this is due to the Russia-Ukraine conflict, however other factors like the pangs of the pandemic, asymmetric and short-term recovery, rising commodity prices and high global inflation have played a role. For India, it is anticipated that the fall in output growth (GDP) could be greater – from 6.7% to 4.6%. This massive fall, more than double the global average is going to be driven by constraints on several fronts including energy access and prices, primary commodity bottlenecks, reflexes from trade sanctions, food inflation, tightening policies and financial instability.
