Editor's Desk
The month of April woke up to the rude shock of a big increase in the retail prices of Petroleum Products, with the price at the pump crossing the psychological INR 100 per liter mark. This increase has also resulted in an upward movement in the inflation rate. In addition to this shock, the heat wave has added fuel to the fire and affected the wheat crop. However, the prediction of another normal monsoon has brought some hope for cheer.

India has amendment its Electricity Rules 2020 and plans to eliminate the use of DG sets in coming 5 years to combat air and noise pollution specially in urban areas.

The big positive news of the month has been the signing of India-Australia Economic Cooperation and Trade Agreement. This is the first trade agreement signed by India with a developed economy after more than a decade.

Amongst other key development, the NITI Aayog has come up with State Energy and Climate index which is the first index that aims to track the efforts made by states and UTs in the climate and energy sector. Gujarat topped this list. A cap on lending limit of NBFCs and a raising of the wage ceiling under EPFO were the other notable developments. The release of the draft battery swapping policy for electric vehicles by NITI Aayog is expected to further drive the cause of electric mobility in the country. At the State level, the Delhi Government has come up with a roadmap for 100 per cent delivery electrification in the state.

The International Monetary Fund's latest World Economic Outlook has rolled back growth prospects for the world and for India. India is expected to grow at 8.2% (highlighted in green) in 2022 and this is by far the best growth rate across all the major economies in the world. It is more than double the average growth rate of both the advanced economies (3.3%) as well as the emerging economies (3.8%). It recommended monetary tightening by central banks to keep inflationary expectations in check amid global supply disruptions caused by the war in Ukraine. The IMF warned that the war would “severely set back the global recovery," slow growth and stoke inflation.

A sudden surge in India’s exports of goods and services in 2021–22 after registering a decline for two consecutive years has helped building positive momentum for the Indian economy. While the exports of goods zoomed by 43.8% to reach an all-time high of $419.7 billion in 2021–22, that of services rose by 20.9% to touch $249.2 billion. The total exports of goods and services were valued at a thumping $668.9 billion after posting a 34.3% growth in 2021–22. Despite the recent gains, the relative size of the exports sector remains modest. Many believe that the surge in Indian exports is mainly due to global factors and not the Government’s trade policy.
